### 2.6 Article Structure & Key Sections

**Introduction (200 words):**
- Melanoma statistics (most dangerous skin cancer, 16,000+ diagnosed annually in NSW)
- Survival directly linked to early detection (98% 5-year survival if caught at stage 1A)
- Importance of specialist dermatology surveillance for high-risk individuals
- Article overview: comprehensive detection guide beyond basic information

**Section 1: Understanding Melanoma (300 words)**
- What is melanoma (melanocyte cancer)
- Four main types:
  - Superficial spreading (70%)
  - Nodular (15% - most aggressive)
  - Lentigo maligna (10%)
  - Acral lentiginous (5% - hands/feet)
- Amelanotic melanoma (non-pigmented, often missed)
- How melanoma differs from other skin cancers
- Why specialist detection matters

**Section 2: The ABCDE Rule and Beyond (350 words)**
- **Classic ABCDE:**
  - Asymmetry
  - Border irregularity
  - Colour variation
  - Diameter >6mm
  - Evolving (changes over time)
- **Additional warning signs:**
  - Ugly duckling sign (looks different from other moles)
  - Bleeding or oozing
  - Itching or tenderness
  - Rapid growth
  - Nodular melanoma exception (EFG rule: Elevated, Firm, Growing)
- **Limitations of self-examination:** Need for specialist expertise

**Section 3: Advanced Detection: Dermoscopy Technology (300 words)**
- What is dermoscopy (magnification + polarized light)
- How dermoscopy improves diagnostic accuracy (30% increase vs. naked eye)
- Dermoscopic patterns suspicious for melanoma
- Digital dermoscopy and mole mapping
- Artificial intelligence integration (emerging technology)
- Why dermatologists are trained in dermoscopy interpretation

**Section 4: Who Needs Specialist Melanoma Surveillance (350 words)**
- **High-risk factors:**
  - Personal history of melanoma
  - Family history (first-degree relative with melanoma)
  - Multiple atypical moles (dysplastic naevi syndrome)
  - 50+ moles total
  - Fair skin, red/blonde hair, blue/green eyes
  - History of severe sunburns (especially childhood)
  - Immunosuppression (transplant recipients, medications)
  - Genetic syndromes (familial atypical mole melanoma syndrome)
- **Surveillance frequency:** Annual vs. 6-monthly vs. 3-monthly (risk-stratified)
- **Digital mole mapping benefits:** Baseline documentation, change detection over time

**Section 5: What to Expect at Specialist Melanoma Screening (300 words)**
- Comprehensive full-body skin examination (including scalp, between toes, nails)
- Risk assessment and family history
- Dermoscopy of suspicious lesions
- Digital mole mapping (if high-risk)
- Biopsy procedure if concerning lesion (shave vs. excisional)
- Pathology timeline (5-7 days typically)
- Follow-up and surveillance plan

**Section 6: Self-Examination Between Specialist Checks (250 words)**
- How to perform monthly self-checks
- Using a mirror or partner assistance
- Photographing concerning moles (for change comparison)
- What changes warrant urgent specialist review
- Self-exam as complement (not replacement) to specialist screening

**Section 7: If Melanoma is Diagnosed (250 words)**
- Staging and prognosis (depth/Breslow thickness critical)
- Wide local excision
- Sentinel lymph node biopsy (when indicated)
- Multidisciplinary care (surgical oncology, medical oncology, radiation oncology)
- Surveillance protocols post-treatment
- Family screening recommendations (genetic risk)

**Section 8: Prevention Strategies (200 words)**
- Sun protection (despite moles being genetic risk)
- UV exposure reduction
- Avoiding tanning beds (melanoma risk increases 75% with use before age 35)
- Vitamin D balance
- Regular specialist surveillance

**Conclusion & CTA (200 words):**
- Early detection saves lives (key message)
- Specialist dermatology expertise critical for high-risk individuals
- Don't delay concerning changes
- Annual skin checks for everyone, more frequent for high-risk
